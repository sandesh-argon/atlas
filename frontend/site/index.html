The Methodology

These are not correlations.

Every connection in Atlas passed five tests before it was included. Not one. Five. Here's what that means.

01
We checked if one thing happens before another.
Granger Causality Prefiltering · FDR q < 0.05

We tested 2,159,672 variable pairs across up to 5 years of lag. 564,545 survived. Those are the ones where cause consistently comes before effect.

02
We removed false connections.
PC-Stable Algorithm + DAG Enforcement · alpha = 0.001

85,726 edges passed the causality test. We then removed circular logic. If A causes B causes A, neither is trustworthy. 58,837 remained.

03
We stress-tested every connection 100 times.
LassoCV + OLS Bootstrap Validation · 100 iterations

Each edge was re-estimated 100 times on different data samples. Only edges with a consistent effect size, every single time, survived. 4,976 out of 58,837 made the cut.

04
We looked for curves, not just straight lines.
Non-Linear Relationship Detection · AIC + R² Threshold

Some effects slow down. Some reverse. Some only appear after a tipping point. We tested five relationship types for every edge: linear, logarithmic, quadratic, saturation, and threshold.

05
We did this for every year, not just once.
Temporal Re-Estimation · 1990–2024 · 5,054 Graph Estimations

The same 7,368 edges are re-estimated for every year, updating their strength, direction, and shape. 35 years. 178 countries. 5,054 total graph snapshots.

What We Found

Four findings that challenge what we thought we knew.

We didn't go looking for these. The data found them.

Education

When girls close the college gap, birth rates fall. Except in rich countries.

College Enrollment Gender Gap → Birth Rate · 140/140 graphs · 35/35 years

In developing and emerging economies, closing the gender gap in higher education consistently drives birth rates down, strongly, year after year. In advanced economies, the same relationship flips positive. The same policy. Opposite outcomes. Where a country sits in its development journey changes everything.

Welfare

Government benefits don't create dependency. They build wealth.

Average Adult Government Benefits → Accumulated Income Per Person · β≈0.98 · Zero uncertainty flags

This is the most consistent finding in the entire dataset. Government benefits are among the strongest upstream predictors of accumulated wealth: in developing countries, emerging economies, and advanced nations alike. The beta is nearly identical across all four strata. This relationship does not change by income level, decade, or region.

Inequality

Growing the middle class helps poor countries grow. It doesn't help rich ones.

Middle Class Income Share → Economic Output Per Person · 140/140 graphs · 35/35 years

In developing economies, expanding the middle class is one of the strongest drivers of economic output. In emerging and advanced economies, the same relationship turns negative. The policy implication is stark: redistribution strategies that work in one context can actively harm growth in another.

Spending

How you spend matters more than how much you spend.

Average Spending per Person → Pre-tax Government Income → Agricultural Income Distribution · Direct edge: 0/140 · Indirect path: 140/140

Spending per person has no direct effect on how income is distributed in agriculture. Zero. But route that spending through government income structures first, and the effect is strong and consistent everywhere. The mechanism is invisible unless you look for it. Most models don't.

None of these were hypotheses. Every one is an edge in the graph, validated 100 times, across 35 years of real data.
